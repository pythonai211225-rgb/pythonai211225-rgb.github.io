Example 1 — Fraud Detection

Duplicate Refund Detection

The finance team noticed refund totals are higher than expected. Your job: find every order that was refunded more than once — the same deal, paid back twice.

The Question

Business Question
🔍 Which orders appear in the refunds table more than once? Show the order ID, the customer who placed it, and how many times it was refunded.

A legitimate order should only be refunded once. If the same order_id appears twice in the refunds table, it means the customer — or a bug — submitted the same refund claim twice. That's a duplicate and it needs to be caught.

The path to get from a refund to a customer name is: refunds → orders → customers

Think It Through — Step by Step

1
What are we counting?
We want to count how many times each order_id appears in the refunds table. A count of 2 or more means the same order was refunded more than once.
2
Which column do we GROUP BY?
Group by order_id — not customer_id. We're looking for duplicate refunds on the same order, not multiple orders by the same customer.
3
How do we filter to only the duplicates?
HAVING COUNT(*) > 1 — this runs after GROUP BY, so it only keeps groups where the order appeared more than once. WHERE can't do this because it filters rows before grouping.
4
How do we get the customer name?
The refunds table only has order_id. To reach the customer we need two JOINs: refunds → orders (to get customer_id), then orders → customers (to get the name).

Step 1 — Find the Duplicate Orders

Start simple — just group by order_id and count. No JOIN yet. Confirm the right orders come back.

SQL
SELECT   order_id,
         COUNT(*) AS refund_count
FROM     refunds
GROUP BY order_id
HAVING   COUNT(*) > 1
ORDER BY refund_count DESC;
Result
order_idrefund_count
1052
1182
✅ Orders 101, 112, and 122 are gone — they only had one refund each. Orders 105 and 118 are our suspects.

Step 2 — Join to Get Customer Details

Now trace the path refunds → orders → customers with two JOINs to show who placed the suspicious orders.

SQL
SELECT   c.name,
         c.email,
         r.order_id,
         COUNT(*) AS refund_count,
         ROUND(SUM(r.amount), 2) AS total_refunded
FROM     refunds r
JOIN     orders o   ON o.id  = r.order_id
JOIN     customers c ON c.id = o.customer_id
GROUP BY r.order_id
HAVING   COUNT(*) > 1
ORDER BY total_refunded DESC;
Result

Sarah Kimsarah@email.com1182178.00
David Levidavid@email.com105299.98
⚠️ Sarah's order 118 was refunded twice — she received 178.00 instead of 89.00. David's order 105 was also double-refunded: 99.98 instead of 49.99.

Key Takeaways

💡

GROUP BY the right column

We grouped by order_id, not customer_id. The question is "same deal refunded twice" — that means the same order, not the same customer across different orders.

💡

HAVING, not WHERE

You cannot write WHERE COUNT(*) > 1 — WHERE filters rows before grouping happens. HAVING runs after GROUP BY, so it can filter on aggregate results like COUNT.

💡

Two JOINs to reach the customer

The refunds table doesn't store the customer name — only the order_id. You must bridge through orders first: refunds → orders → customers.

A refund cannot happen before the order was placed. But order 203 was placed on Feb 14 and refunded on Feb 9 — five days earlier. Order 205 was placed on Feb 18 and refunded on Feb 15 — three days before the purchase. These timestamps are impossible and signal either a bug or data tampering.
